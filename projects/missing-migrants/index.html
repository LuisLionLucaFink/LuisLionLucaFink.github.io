Mapping the Invisible

Spatial Patterns of Death and Missing Along International Migration Routes

Overview

This dashboard visualizes migrant deaths and disappearances across the globe. It highlights major migration corridors and uses spatial analysis to uncover meaningful geographic and temporal patterns. The visualizations aim to support humanitarian understanding through interactive maps and clustering techniques.

← Back to portfolio

Motivation

Every year, thousands of migrants risk their lives crossing international borders. Many do not survive. While these tragedies are often reduced to numbers, this project uses data to identify where and when these deaths and disappearances happen, across multiple major migration routes.

By uncovering spatial and temporal patterns in migrant fatalities across the top six most dangerous migration corridors, including the Mediterranean, the US Mexico border, the Sahara Desert and others, this analysis aims to support evidence based action. The goal is not just to visualize loss, but to reveal where preventive measures could save the most lives.

Data Source

The data is provided by the International Organization for Migration through the Missing Migrants Project. It documents deaths and disappearances during migration worldwide. The dataset includes time, location, demographics, and cause of death. It spans from 2014 to the present and is continuously updated by NGOs, media, and official sources.

Dataset download: missingmigrants.iom.int/downloads

Global Heatmap of Incidents

To provide an initial spatial overview, we created a global heatmap that shows where migrant deaths and disappearances have occurred since 2014. Each data point is weighted by the number of dead and missing persons to emphasize severity.

This visualization helps identify high risk zones and geographic concentrations of migrant fatalities. It serves as a baseline for more detailed route specific analyses that follow.

Clear global patterns emerge from this heatmap: high densities are visible in North Africa, the Central and Eastern Mediterranean, the US Mexico border region, and the Afghanistan Iran corridor, highlighting where deadly risks are most acute.

Exploratory Visualizations: Demographics, Origins, Causes and Routes

To understand the underlying patterns in migrant deaths, we first explore descriptive statistics and visualizations based on the full dataset. These include demographic breakdowns, top countries of origin, the most common causes of death, and the relative frequency and danger of different migration routes. These insights help lay the foundation for the spatial analyses that follow.

Demographic Profile of the Deceased: Demographic Distribution

The vast majority of recorded deaths involve men (66.3 percent), followed by women (20.9 percent) and children (12.9 percent). This distribution reflects broader global migration patterns, where adult males are more likely to undertake risky routes. However, the substantial proportion of women and children highlights growing vulnerabilities across migrant groups, particularly in family based or forced displacements.

Percentage distribution of deaths and missing by demographic group.
Geographic Origins of Migrants: Top 10 Countries of Origin

The country of origin offers important context for understanding migratory push factors. A significant portion of the data is marked as “Unknown”, underscoring the frequent lack of identification during fatal incidents. Among identifiable cases, countries like Afghanistan (17 percent) and Mexico (8 percent) are most represented, followed by a variety of African and Central American nations. These patterns correspond to regions affected by conflict, instability, and poverty.

Causes of Death: Top 6

By far the most frequent cause of death is drowning (57.5 percent), highlighting the extreme risks of maritime crossings, especially in the Mediterranean and Atlantic. A considerable portion of cases are labeled as mixed or unknown causes (14.5 percent), revealing gaps in incident reporting. Other major contributors include hazardous transport accidents (8.9 percent), violence (6.8 percent), and harsh environmental conditions (6.8 percent) such as dehydration, desert exposure, and extreme temperatures, particularly on land routes through the Sahara.

Migration Routes: Comparing Exposure and Fatality

Routes by reported incidents. This bar chart shows the most frequently reported migration routes in terms of number of incidents. The US Mexico border leads with approximately 35 percent of all reported incidents, followed by the Afghanistan Iran corridor and Sahara Desert crossings. However, this chart only reflects incident frequency, not the severity of each incident. It is important to note that a single recorded incident may involve one individual or many people. For example, an incident can represent a large-scale boat disaster resulting in hundreds of deaths, or a single migrant who dies during a desert crossing.

Routes by deaths and missing. When shifting the focus to the actual number of deaths and disappearances, a stark contrast becomes evident: the Mediterranean route emerges as the deadliest by far, accounting for over 50 percent of all recorded deaths globally. Other high risk corridors include the Sahara Desert (11 percent), US Mexico border (10 percent), Canary Islands route (7.7 percent), Eastern Route to or from EHOA (6.8 percent), and the Afghanistan Iran corridor (5 percent).

The observed differences between migration routes based on incident counts versus total deaths and missing persons are largely explained by the nature of incidents on each route: At the US–Mexico border, many incidents involve individual crossings, where typically one person is recorded as missing or deceased. In contrast, Mediterranean incidents often involve entire boats capsizing, where a single incident can result in dozens or even hundreds of deaths at once, due to overcrowded vessels attempting dangerous sea crossings.

Selected Routes for Deeper Analysis
Analytical focus:
While incident-based visualizations provide insight into the frequency of reported events, they do not capture the severity of migration routes.

For this reason, the subsequent analyses focus on migration corridors ranked by the number of deaths and missing persons, which provide a more meaningful measure of humanitarian impact.

In detail, it concentrates on six major corridors that together account for over 92 percent of global recorded deaths: the Mediterranean routes (Central, Western, Eastern), the Sahara Desert, the US–Mexico border, the Canary Islands route, the Eastern Route to or from EHOA, and the Afghanistan–Iran corridor. These corridors were selected due to their high fatality burden, geographic diversity, and representativeness of broader global migration patterns.
Temporal and Spatial-Temporal Patterns

In this step, we examine how migrant deaths and disappearances evolve over time. The analysis focuses on both monthly and yearly temporal patterns across the six selected migration corridors, providing insight into seasonal dynamics as well as longer-term trends. In addition, these temporal patterns form the basis for subsequent spatial-temporal analyses, where shifts in the geographic center of incidents are explored.

Monthly Patterns

When aggregating deaths and missing by month, clear seasonal variations become apparent, particularly along maritime routes. The Mediterranean corridor consistently records the highest number of fatalities throughout the year, with pronounced peaks during the spring and early summer months, especially in April, May, and June. These periods correspond to calmer sea conditions, during which a higher number of crossings are attempted, often in overcrowded and unsafe vessels.

Other routes, such as the Sahara Desert, the Canary Islands, and the Eastern Route to or from EHOA, exhibit more moderate seasonal fluctuations. In contrast, the US–Mexico border shows a relatively even distribution of deaths and missing across the year. This reflects fundamental differences in migration mechanisms: while sea-based crossings are highly dependent on weather and seasonal conditions, land-based routes are influenced by a broader range of social, political, and enforcement-related factors and are less directly tied to climate seasonality.

Yearly Patterns

Examining the data on a yearly basis reveals distinct longer-term trends across the selected migration corridors. Deaths along the Mediterranean routes peaked sharply between 2015 and 2016, followed by a substantial decline until around 2020. In more recent years, particularly between 2022 and 2023, fatalities have increased again, indicating a renewed intensification of risk along this corridor.

The Canary Islands route and the Afghanistan–Iran corridor display a different trajectory, with marked increases in deaths and disappearances after 2020. These trends suggest shifting migration pressures and route adaptations in response to geopolitical developments, border controls, and regional instability. In contrast, the Sahara Desert and the US–Mexico border routes remain comparatively stable over time, showing fluctuations but no dramatic peaks comparable to those observed in the Mediterranean.
